Brand Canvas

*StoryBrand framework for messaging and positioning*

1. The Character (The Customer)

*The customer is the hero of this story. Define the ideal customer by
their role, responsibilities, desires, and needs.*

**Primary Hero: CRO / CCO / VP Sales**

**Who they are:** Revenue leader at a EUR10-250M ARR B2B SaaS scale-up.
Owns the number. Reports to CEO and board. Has 50-1,500 employees
depending on them to execute.

**What they are responsible for:**

-   Hitting the growth story promised to investors

-   Defending the forecast every quarter

-   Aligning marketing, sales, and CS around a single pipeline

-   Making resource allocation decisions without perfect information

**What they want:**

-   To walk into board meetings with calm confidence, not anxious hope

-   To know exactly where deals are leaking and fix them in days

-   To sleep at night knowing the system works

-   To be known as the CRO who built something repeatable

2. The Problem

*What is the main problem your customer experiences?*

**External Problem**

*The concrete problem they are trying to solve:*

**"Simple revenue questions take hours or days to answer, spread across
multiple Excel files."**

-   Forecasts nobody trusts: numbers change week to week

-   Handoffs leak everywhere: MQL to SQL, Closed to Onboarded, Active to
    Expanding

-   CRM is noisy or half-empty; reps do not trust it, leadership cannot
    use it

-   Board prep means jumping between multiple spreadsheets for hours

-   Each function has their own dashboard and their own story about why
    they missed

-   Pipeline coverage looks fine but they miss anyway

-   Zombie deals clogging the pipeline

**Internal Problem**

*The emotional or psychological effect:*

**"I dread board meetings. Will the numbers hold? Will I be blamed when
they do not?"**

-   Exhausted from constant firefighting instead of building

-   Imposter syndrome: maybe I am just not good at this

-   Loneliness at the top: cannot admit how much they are winging it

-   Frustration that adding more people and tools did not fix it

-   Fear that they will be replaced when the miss happens

**Philosophical Problem**

*The deeper moral problem that makes this feel wrong:*

**"Revenue should not be a guessing game. We have all this data. Why
can we not answer simple questions?"**

-   I should not need heroics every quarter. That is not sustainable.

-   The best companies do not operate like this. Why do we?

-   There should be a system. Not opinions and spreadsheets.

3. The Big Hairy Monster

*Name the enemy. What are you fighting against?*

**"Heroics-dependent growth"**

The belief that revenue comes from individual superstars pulling off
miracles, instead of from a system that reliably produces outcomes. The
villain is the firefighting culture: the idea that last-minute
scrambles, hero reps, and "just try harder" are normal ways to run a
business.

*Alternative villain names:*

-   Spreadsheet chaos

-   The black box

-   Founder heroics

4. The Guide

*Position yourself as the guide who can help the hero.*

**Empathy**

*Shared experiences that show understanding:*

-   Sitting in the seat where people disappear into Excel for hours
    when the board asks a simple question.

-   Seeing companies blame the CRM, blame the people, blame the
    departments. The real problem was always the system.

-   Working with scale-ups where "hire more" was the only lever
    they knew, and watching efficiency crater.

-   Knowing what it feels like when nobody trusts the numbers and every
    forecast feels like a guess.

**Authority**

*Proof the problem can actually be solved:*

-   15+ years helping B2B and technology companies turn scattered data
    into shipped changes that show up in pipeline, forecasts, and NRR

-   Background: data → technology and process → digital transformation → strategy-to-execution

-   Always an operator, never just an advisor from the side. Sitting with
    teams and implementing, not just consulting

-   Lean Black Belt trained: operational efficiency from heavy industry
    applied to SaaS

-   Winning by Design revenue architecture methodology

-   Focus: sustainable change, not quick wins. Implementing impact, not
    decks.

5. The Plan (The Steps)

*Clear, simple steps that show how to get from problem to solution.*

**Step 1: Find the Real Constraint**

-   Run a quick maturity scan across four capabilities: Customer,
    Revenue Flow, Enablement, Governance

-   Map current state to the bowtie and identify where value leaks

-   Get a 1-page Focus Map with the real constraint and first move

**Step 2: Install the Structure**

-   Build a One-Page Plan: goals, targets, programmes with clear owners

-   Stand up a Revenue Room: single view of metrics, decisions, and
    experiments

-   Launch an Operating Cadence: weekly rhythm that turns data into
    decisions

-   Start first experiments by week 3

**Step 3: Run and Improve**

-   Transfer ownership to internal RevOps team

-   Continue with fractional support if needed

-   Run quarterly health checks and tune the system

6. The Promise

*What can a customer expect? The more concrete, the better.*

**Concrete Promises**

-   Answer simple revenue questions in minutes, not days

-   Defend the forecast in 10 minutes with data everyone trusts

-   Find pipeline leaks in days, not quarters

-   One Room, one truth, one rhythm

-   First experiments running by week 3

**Emotional Promises**

-   Walk into board meetings with calm confidence, not anxious hope

-   Sleep better knowing the system works, not just hoping the team pulls it off

-   RevOps becomes the hero who owns the system, not the report factory

7. Call to Action

*What specific action should the customer take?*

**Direct CTAs**

-   Book a Focus Audit (primary: low friction, proves value fast)

-   Take the Revenue Engine Checkup (10-minute diagnostic + 45-minute
    working session)

**Transitional CTAs**

-   Follow for thought leadership content (content funnel)

-   Download the Revenue Engine Maturity Framework (lead magnet)

-   Join a Revenue Room Workshop (event)

8. The Positive Outcome (Success)

*What does the hero's life look like after they have completed the
journey?*

**External Success**

-   Forecasts you can defend in front of your board and investors

-   Pipeline leaks you can find and fix in days instead of weeks

-   NRR that compounds because teams pull in the same direction

-   One Room where leadership runs the revenue conversation

-   Win rates improving because you know where to focus

**Internal Success**

-   Calm confidence instead of anxious hope

-   Sleeping through the night because the system works

-   Building something repeatable instead of firefighting

-   Credibility with board and investors restored

**Philosophical Success**

-   Revenue is not a guessing game anymore. There is a system.

-   All that data finally answers simple questions.

-   The company operates like the best companies do.

9. The Negative Outcome (Failure)

*What does the hero miss if they do not act? Create urgency.*

-   Another quarter of forecasts nobody trusts and surprised board
    members

-   "Hire more" as the only lever, while efficiency keeps dropping

-   CAC payback stretching until investors lose patience

-   RevOps stays a report factory; the system never gets fixed

-   The next CRO gets hired to clean up the mess

-   Hoping the team will pull it off until they do not

10. The Transformation

*How does the hero see themselves now vs. how they want to see
themselves?*

**FROM:**

-   Spreadsheet hostage

-   Pipeline firefighter

-   Anxious improviser

-   Hoping team pulls it off

-   CRO who left a mess

**TO:**

-   Revenue architect

-   System builder

-   Calm defender of numbers

-   Knowing the system works

-   CRO who built something repeatable

**One-Line Transformation:**

*"You go from spreadsheet hostage and pipeline firefighter to architect
of a revenue system that reliably hits or de-risks the number."*
